## TL;DR

We prove that **RLHF on a Bradley-Terry reward model**, in the limit of large preference
datasets and infinite policy capacity, is **mathematically equivalent** to behavioral
cloning on the implicit "annotator preference policy." This holds for both PPO and DPO.

## Why this matters

A lot of alignment thinking treats RLHF as fundamentally different from supervised
fine-tuning. Under the standard preference-model assumption, it is not: the gradient
points to the same fixed point.

## Three concrete consequences

1. **Diversity collapse is structural**, not a tuning bug. SFT-style mode-seeking is
   built into the objective.
2. **Reward hacking** can be analyzed as out-of-distribution behavioral cloning —
   recasting it as an iid-violation problem.
3. **Iterated DPO** does not gain you fresh information after the first pass; the
   improvement is purely from better optimization, not from "extra alignment."

## What our framework does *not* claim

We are not saying RLHF is useless. SFT and RLHF agree at the limit; in finite-data
regimes they have very different bias-variance tradeoffs, and RLHF often wins.

## Empirical sanity checks

We replicate three known RLHF anomalies (length bias, sycophancy, alignment-tax) as
predicted artifacts of the equivalence.
